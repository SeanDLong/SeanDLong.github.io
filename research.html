White Identity, Violence, and the 2016 Election

Working Paper

This paper seeks to examine the relationship between Donald Trump's 2016 election, the alt-right, and white identity. Specifically, it seeks to understand why white extremists broke with long-held precedent against supporting major politicians post-Civil Rights. This paper will explore how Trump's candidacy mobilized members of the white extremist alt-right to engage further with mainstream politics. This will be shown through the use of interrupted time series analyses on blog texts from the prominent , using unsupervised learning to discerm topic selection from that text. Then, I will perform both an OLS regression and a mediation analysis on 2018 ANES Pilot data to explore how white identity influences support for the alt-right, which in turn predicts positive evaluations of Donald Trump. Overall, this paper shows how the salience of white identity mobilized extremist groups to engage in more mainstream political action, including overt support for Trump and efforts to appear more mainstream in tone.

This project is currently being expanded into my dissertation.

How the 2016 election hardened conservative discourse on immigration

Working Paper

There has been a recent increase in scholarly attention to the racialization of American public opinion and the role of fragmentation in media studies. This paper seeks to track how Donald Trump's campaign for president in 2015 and 2016 impacted right-wing media discourse over immigration. Specifically, we show that support for Trump by various media outlets consistently accompanied increasingly hostile narratives surrounding immigration. This effect proved to hold across the right-wing spectrum, including both mainstream and extreme news sources. We use a combination of text-based machine learning techniques to develop models of media trends for both extremist and more mainstream conservative news organizations. By collecting thousands of blog posts and news articles from the white supremacist Daily Stormer, and conservative news outlets Breitbart News and Fox News we were able to show that the Trump campaign essentially united previously fragmented media sources around similar anti-immigrant frames.

This project is being completed alongside Dr. Loren Collingwood

Implicit and Explicit White Identity Cues

Working Paper

Recent research has found that, despite a long-standing inclination for politicians to use implicit rather than explicit racial cues, explicit cues are becoming increasingly common in political discourse. Combined with the increased salience of white identity in American politicis, this project explores whether White voters are receptive to implicit or explicit appeals to threats to white identity and explores the connection between such appeals and White attitudes towards violence and immigration.

This project is being completed alongside Dr. Charles Crabtree

Tactical Gun Stores, White Identity, and Race in America

Working Paper

With the rise of White violence and armed militia groups in contemporary American politics, this project attempts to understand the cultural role of tactical gun stores, i.e. gun stores that focus on the sale of tactical weapons and other gear. Using novel data collection from Google Maps API to identify the locations of different types of gun stores, we hope to further understand what this heavily militarized aspect of gun culture tells us about contemporary White violence.

This project is being completed alongside Dr. Ashley Jardina and Dr. Loren Collingwood

Women and Political Ambition: Running for Us or Running for Me?

Working Paper

The year 2018 has been dubbed the year of the woman because of the increased number of women not only running for office, but also winning. For the first time in U.S. history, over 100 women are in the U.S. House, with 32 being newly elected. The freshmen class is also among the most diverse in U.S. history. While the national level has received the lion’s share of attention in the media, similar trends have been happening at the state and local level. What helps to account for this dramatic increase?

This project is being completed alongside the UCR Gender Lab
